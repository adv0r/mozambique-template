LAND AQUISITION

When talking about ‘large-scale land acquisitions’, investors adopt optimistic terms like ‘development opportunity’ or ‘win-win perspective’.
On the other hand, civil society labels it negatively, defying it as land grabbing. In the mid, is the academic world of research, that has a more neutral approach to the phenomenon.
Read the IIED report

The International Land Coalition’s Tirana Declaration defines ‘land grabbing’ as


Acquisitions or concessions that are one or more of the following:



In violation of human rights, particularly the equal rights of women;

Not based on free, prior and informed consent of the affected land users;

Not based on a through assessment, or are in disregard of social, economic and environmental impacts, including the way they are gendered;

Not based on transparent contracts that specify clear and binding commitments about activities, employment and benefits sharing;

Not based on effective democratic planning, independent oversight and meaningful participation.

LAND GRABBING DEFINITION

How many hectares have been leased, in total, since the land rush began?
As land grabbing definition is blurred, the same we can say of the physical boundaries of the phenomenon. How many hectares have been leased, in total, since the rush to land begun? Any attempt of outlining the actual extension of the large-scale land acquisition will provide a confusinonary set of self-contradicting numbers. The World Bank speaks of 56 million hectares having been leased just from 2008 to 2009 as a consequence of the food-price dropping. Different numbers are given by many sources, while coming by a truth becomes an impossible task. The International Food Policy Institute and the Oakland Institute have counted about 15-20 million hectares leased from 2006 to 2009. In 2011 Land Matrix, a database of large-scale land acquisitions developed by four international research institutes, calculated 227 million hectares of land being leased from the year 2000 onwards. A consistent number, put together by sources that are not always reliable, and which in June 2013, after a re-launch of the database, was downsized to 33 million hectares as a reliable number taken only from signed contracts. That is an area eight times the size of the Netherlands. “It is impossible to state with total certainty that a land acquisition really took place, having which exact surface, which usage and for how much time it is given in concession,” state the researchers of Land Matrix. “Because some projects begin, and then they run into different problems and are discontinued and close down, or on the contrary they expand beyond the contracted surfaces.”
